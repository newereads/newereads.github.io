The Rules
The content constitution. If it follows the rules, post it. Full stop.
Always
✦
End every post with a real, specific question. The community is built in the comments.
✦
Wise older sister, not expert. "I needed this book at this exact moment" — not "you should read this."
✦
Always dress the part. Parisian autumn. Yellow is an accent, not the palette.
✦
Always protect the other story. His privacy is sacred. "I felt" — not "he did."
✦
Specificity over relatability. "I read this at 1am with cold tea" beats "this book destroyed me."
✦
If it stops feeling like joy, we change it. This account exists to give back to her.
Never
×
Never post in the heat of a hard day. Wait 24 hours. Your audience deserves the processed version.
×
Never subtweet or vague-post. If you can't name it directly, don't post about it.
×
Never sacrifice the aesthetic for a mediocre photo. A gap in the feed is better than an off-brand post.
×
Never review a book you didn't connect with just for content. Your taste is your credibility.
×
Never let ARC pressure compromise your honesty. Kind and honest. Never dishonest and kind.
×
Never post your body in a way that doesn't feel like you. Fashion because you love clothes — never as currency.

·

·

·
Any post written from inside a spiral vs. from the other side of one

Posting cadence — the honest data
What the research actually says
✦
3 posts/week is the move. Accounts posting 3–5x/week more than double follower growth vs. 1–2x. Your friend is right that 2x is the floor — not the target.
✦
But quality over volume. Instagram 2026 ranks by "sends per reach" — how often people DM your post to a friend. One post that makes someone say "I need to send this" beats five generic posts every time.
✦
Best posting times: Tuesday 1–7pm, Wednesday 12–9pm, Thursday 12–2pm. Midweek afternoons. Not weekends, not mornings.
✦
Carousels outperform everything on engagement. Your Fashion x Books paired looks and FMC deep dives should almost always be carousels.
·
The verdict on your friend: Listen — but on your terms. Move from 2 to 3 posts/week. Make one of those a Story-heavy day with no feed post. Do not go daily until you have a full content engine running.
Your weekly sprint template
Tuesday: Fashion x Books carousel (peak engagement day, lead with your strongest content)
Thursday: Reviews + Recs or Lifestyle (people reading during their commute home)
Sunday: Community post — Trope Tuesday, poll, or BookTok Made Me Read It (Sunday scroll energy)
Daily Stories: Behind the scenes, current read check-in, poll, share a post from a creator you love
Batch shoot: One Sunday per month. 3–4 hours = 4–6 weeks of visual content.
Automatable and repeatable (things to pull or set up)
🔗
Linktree or Beacons: Set up once, update weekly. Link to Bookshop.org affiliate list, LTK, latest post, and Goodreads. This is your income infrastructure from day 1.
📅
Later or Buffer (free tier): Schedule all 3 weekly posts in one sitting on Sunday. Never post in the moment for feed content — schedule everything so you can show up fresh in comments.
📊
Weekly Instagram Insights check (10 min every Monday): Top post by saves, top post by reach, follower growth, story views. Saves = content people want to return to. That is your north star metric.
📚
Goodreads export (monthly): Go to My Books → Import/Export → Export Library → paste the CSV here. I will map your reads to content pillars, draft review captions, and flag ARC-adjacent authors automatically.
🔔
Author release alerts: Follow your priority authors on Goodreads and set notifications. The hub release radar covers July–November — update it here each quarter.
💰
LTK + Bookshop.org: Apply to both before your first post. Even with 0 followers you can generate affiliate links. Every Fashion x Books post should have a linked outfit. Every review should link to Bookshop.

Series format — how to post it
The post: Photo of the finished bake. Warm light, cream surface, one character-coded prop (a book, a record, a candle in their palette color).
The caption: What you made. What character. What you were listening to. What the book made you feel. End with a recipe question or a "which character are you baking as today?"
Stories: The process — the mixing bowl, the oven timer, the first slice. Stories are where the baking series lives fully. Feed is just the hero shot.
The series name: "Baking to [Book Title]" or "Midnight Bakes with [Character]" — keep it consistent so it becomes findable.
